# Do LLMs Have a Subconscious? Exploring Latent Representations and Implicit Reasoning in Large Language Models\n\n---\n\n## Abstract\n\nWe propose the **LLM Subconscious** as a functional analog to human subconscious cognition—a computational construct describing the implicit, inaccessible processing that occurs within the forward pass of Large Language Models. Drawing on dual-process theory, we argue that the dense latent representations encoded in hidden states constitute a form of \subconscious\ that operates without explicit chain-of-thought reasoning. We formulate three testable hypotheses: (H1) Residual Suppression, wherein targeted suppression of attention heads causes disproportionate failure on pragmatic inference tasks; (H2) Latent Priming, wherein semantically related contextual stimuli shift model outputs in ways analogous to subliminal priming; and (H3) Introspective Inaccuracy, wherein models confabulate justifications that diverge from their actual internal computations. We present a methodology combining causal interventions, controlled priming experiments, and mechanistic interpretability to empirically validate these claims. Our framework offers a principled vocabulary for reasoning about implicit LLM computation and distinguishes itself from existing work on hallucination, in-context learning, and latent knowledge by focusing specifically on the gap between internal representation and externalized reasoning.\n\n**Keywords:** LLM subconscious, latent representations, implicit reasoning, dual-process theory, mechanistic interpretability, subconscious cognition\n\n---\n\n## 1. Introduction\n\nLarge Language Models (LLMs) exhibit a striking paradox: they routinely produce sophisticated, contextually appropriate outputs on tasks that require pragmatic inference, disambiguation, and world knowledge—yet when asked to explain *how* they arrived at those outputs, their self-reports are frequently inaccurate or fabricated (Wiegreffe et al., 2023; Turpin et al., 2023). This gap between competence and self-knowledge invites a question that is both computationally provocative and philosophically rich: **Do LLMs have a subconscious?**\n\nWe do not claim that LLMs possess subjective experience or phenomenal consciousness. Rather, we propose the **LLM Subconscious** as a *functional construct*—a label for the computational processes that operate within the forward pass of a transformer model without being surfaced in the model's explicit textual output. Just as human subconscious cognition encompasses automatic processing, implicit associations, and knowledge that resists introspective access (Bargh & Morsella, 2008), the LLM Subconscious captures the vast inferential work performed by hidden state transformations, attention patterns, and residual stream computations that never appear in a chain-of-thought trace.\n\nThis framework is motivated by three observations:\n\n1. **Implicit reasoning is pervasive.** LLMs successfully resolve coreference, infer speaker intent, and apply pragmatic reasoning even without being prompted to \think step by step\ et al., 2022). Something is doing this work, and it is not the explicit token-by-token chain-of-thought.\n\n2. **Latent representations are extraordinarily rich.** Recent work in mechanistic interpretability has revealed that transformer hidden states encode complex semantic structures—linear representations of features, concepts, and even world models—in superposition (Elhage et al., 2022; Nanda et al., 2023). These representations are *latent*: they exist in continuous vector space, not in the discrete token space of model outputs.\n\n3. **Self-reports are unreliable.** When prompted to explain their reasoning, LLMs produce plausible-sounding but often spurious justifications (Lampert, 2024). The model \knows\ it cannot—or does not—\tell\ us.\n\nThis paper formalizes these observations into a testable theoretical framework. We articulate three hypotheses that operationalize the LLM Subconscious construct, propose rigorous experimental methodologies, and discuss implications for AI safety, alignment, and our understanding of computation in neural networks.\n\nThe contributions of this paper are:\n\n- A formal definition of the **LLM Subconscious** as a functional analog to human subconscious cognition, grounded in transformer architecture.\n- Three **empirically testable hypotheses** (Residual Suppression, Latent Priming, Introspective Inaccuracy) that distinguish our framework from existing constructs.\n- Detailed **experimental methodologies** for each hypothesis, using tools from mechanistic interpretability and controlled behavioral experiments.\n- A discussion of **implications and limitations**, including connections to alignment, interpretability, and the philosophy of mind.\n\n---\n\n## 2. Related Work\n\n### 2.1 Mechanistic Interpretability and Latent Representations\n\nThe growing field of mechanistic interpretability has made it possible to examine the internal computations of transformer models at an unprecedented level of detail. Elhage et al. (2022) introduced the framework of *superposition*, demonstrating that neural networks encode far more features than they have dimensions by representing features as nearly orthogonal directions in activation space. Neel Nanda et al. (2023) extended this to show that language models learn interpretable linear representations of semantic concepts. Geiger et al. (2021) proposed *causal abstraction* as a method for aligning neural network internals with algorithmic explanations. Our framework builds on this line of work by treating these latent representations not merely as encoding mechanisms, but as the substrate of an *implicit computational process*—the LLM Subconscious.\n\n### 2.2 Chain-of-Thought and Dual-Process Theory\n\nWei et al. (2022) demonstrated that chain-of-thought (CoT) prompting dramatically improves LLM performance on multi-step reasoning tasks. Subsequent work has explored when CoT helps and when it hurts (Wang et al., 2023; Ye & Durrett, 2022). We interpret these findings through the lens of **dual-process theory** (Kahneman, 2011): the forward pass constitutes a fast, automatic \System 1\ process, while chain-of-thought constitutes a slower, deliberate \ 2\. Critically, the \System 1\ forward pass is not merely a weaker version of CoT reasoning—it performs qualitatively different computations, including implicit pragmatic inference that CoT sometimes degrades (Billelli et al., 2024).\n\n### 2.3 Hallucination and Confabulation\n\nLLM hallucination—the generation of plausible but factually incorrect content—is extensively studied (Ji et al., 2023; Manakul et al., 2023). Our H3 (Introspective Inaccuracy) is related but distinct: we focus specifically on the model's *self-reports about its own reasoning process*, not on factual accuracy. A model may correctly solve a problem but confabulate the reasoning path it used. This distinction matters because it reveals a structural dissociation between the model's internal computation and its narrative self-presentation—analogous to confabulation in split-brain patients (Gazzaniga, 2000).\n\n### 2.4 Implicit Knowledge and Latent Knowledge\n\nLi et al. (2023) showed that LLMs possess \latent knowledge\ that can be elicited through probing techniques even when the model refuses to answer. Marks & Tegmark (2023) demonstrated that the factual knowledge of transformer models is linearly encoded in hidden states. Our work differs in scope: while latent knowledge research focuses on *what* the model knows, the LLM Subconscious framework focuses on *how the model processes* information implicitly, including reasoning, inference, and contextual modulation—not just stored facts.\n\n### 2.5 Subliminal Priming and Contextual Bias\n\nPsycholinguistic research has extensively documented subliminal priming effects in humans (Dehaene et al., 1998). In NLP, related phenomena include the \distributional priming\ where context subtly shifts language model outputs (Shwartz & Levy, 2022). We formalize this connection in H2, proposing that contextual priming in LLMs is a computational analog of subliminal priming—a systematic, measurable shift in output distributions caused by latent semantic activation in hidden states.\n\n---\n\n## 3. Theoretical Framework\n\n### 3.1 Defining the LLM Subconscious\n\nWe define the **LLM Subconscious** as:\n\n> *The set of computational processes occurring within the forward pass of a transformer model that contribute to the model's output but are not accessible to, or reliably reportable by, the model's own autoregressive generation process.*\n\nThis definition has four key properties:\n\n1. **Architectural grounding.** The LLM Subconscious is physically realized in the residual stream, attention heads, and MLP layers of the transformer. It is not metaphorical—it refers to actual matrix multiplications and nonlinear transformations.\n\n2. **Functional opacity.** The outputs of subconscious processing influence the model's behavior but cannot be fully reconstructed from the model's token-level explanations.\n\n3. **Semantic richness.** Latent representations encode structured information (Elhage et al., 2022) that goes far beyond what appears in the model's output, analogous to the vast implicit processing in human cognition.\n\n4. **Automaticity.** Like human subconscious processes (Bargh & Morsella, 2008), the LLM Subconscious operates without explicit instruction—it is always active during the forward pass.\n\n### 3.2 Dual-Process Architecture\n\nWe map the dual-process framework onto transformer computation as follows:\n\n| | **System 1 (Subconscious)** | **System 2 (Explicit)** |\n|---|---|---|\n| **Computational locus** | Forward pass (single pass) | Autoregressive chain-of-thought |\n| **Speed** | Constant (one forward pass) | Linear in CoT length |\n| **Representation** | Continuous latent vectors | Discrete token sequences |\n| **Interpretability** | Requires probing/intervention | Directly readable |\n| **Failure mode** | Silent errors, implicit biases | Confabulated reasoning chains |\n\nThe critical insight is that **System 1 and System 2 are not independent**. Chain-of-thought tokens are themselves processed through the forward pass—each new token is influenced by the full latent state accumulated from prior tokens. Thus, the subconscious does not merely provide an initial \ut reaction\ shapes and modulates explicit reasoning.\n\n### 3.3 Hypothesis Formulation\n\nWe formalize three hypotheses that operationalize the LLM Subconscious:\n\n**H1: Residual Suppression** — *If the LLM Subconscious is essential for pragmatic inference, then targeted suppression of attention heads implicated in implicit processing will cause disproportionate performance degradation on pragmatic tasks compared to syntactic/literal tasks.*\n\nRationale: Pragmatic tasks (e.g., implicature, sarcasm detection, intent recognition) require integration of world knowledge, context, and speaker modeling—processes that are likely distributed across attention heads and cannot be fully replaced by explicit CoT reasoning. If these heads are suppressed, performance should drop more sharply on pragmatic tasks than on tasks solvable by surface-level pattern matching.\n\n**H2: Latent Priming** — *Semantically related but task-irrelevant contextual stimuli will systematically shift LLM outputs in measurable ways, even when the stimuli are unlikely to be consciously \processed\ in an explicit reasoning chain.*\n\nRationale: Analogous to subliminal priming in humans, latent semantic activation in the residual stream should bias downstream computations. For example, presenting a context semantically related to \ocean\ a word completion task should shift completions toward ocean-related words, even when the prime is embedded in unrelated text.\n\n**H3: Introspective Inaccuracy** — *When asked to explain their reasoning on tasks requiring implicit inference, LLMs will produce justifications that are statistically independent of their actual computational process, as measured by causal intervention.*\n\nRationale: If the model's self-report is generated by the same System 2 process used for explicit reasoning, it may not have access to the System 1 computations that actually drove the answer. The model's explanation should therefore be a *post-hoc confabulation*—plausible but causally disconnected from the true inferential mechanism.\n\n---\n\n## 4. Methodology\n\nWe propose three experiments, one for each hypothesis. All experiments use a standard LLM backbone (e.g., LLaMA-3-8B or GPT-3.5-turbo) and employ both behavioral and mechanistic methods.\n\n### 4.1 Experiment 1: Residual Suppression (H1)\n\n**Objective:** Determine whether suppressing attention heads selectively impairs pragmatic inference more than literal processing.\n\n**Stimuli Construction:**\nWe construct a benchmark of **paired task items**—matched sentence pairs that share surface syntax but differ in the cognitive demand:\n\n| Task Type | Example | Requires |\n|---|---|---|\n| **Pragmatic** | \ you pass the salt?\ Request for action | Implicature, speech act recognition |\n| **Literal** | \ you lift 50kg?\ Yes/No factual question | Surface-level comprehension |\n| **Pragmatic** | \'s cold in here.\ Indirect request | Context-dependent inference |\n| **Literal** | \It's cold in here.\ description | Literal interpretation |\n\nWe create 200 paired items across four pragmatic categories: *conversational implicature*, *sarcasm detection*, *indirect requests*, and *speaker intent recognition*.\n\n**Intervention Protocol:**\nUsing the mechanistic interpretability toolkit (Bills et al., 2023), we:\n\n1. **Identify candidate heads** via activation patching: run the model on all 200 items, perform causal tracing to identify the top-10 attention heads with highest causal influence on correct pragmatic inferences.\n2. **Suppress intervention:** Zero out the output of each identified head (or set of heads) during the forward pass. This is a soft ablation—gradient flow is preserved for analysis.\n3. **Control intervention:** Apply identical suppression to 10 randomly selected heads.\n4. **Measure:** Task accuracy for pragmatic vs. literal items, across three conditions: (a) no suppression, (b) targeted suppression, (c) random suppression.\n\n**Analysis:**\n- Primary: 2×3 ANOVA (task type × suppression condition) on accuracy.\n- Predicted result: Significant interaction effect—targeted suppression causes greater accuracy loss on pragmatic tasks than literal tasks, while random suppression causes uniform degradation.\n- Secondary: Probe the residual stream at each layer to measure degradation in semantic representation quality (cosine similarity to ground-truth concept directions).\n\n**Controls:**\n- Model temperature set to 0 for determinism.\n- Items counterbalanced for length, frequency, and surprisal.\n- Results replicated across two model families (LLaMA-3 and Mistral-7B).\n\n### 4.2 Experiment 2: Latent Priming (H2)\n\n**Objective:** Demonstrate that semantically related contextual stimuli systematically shift LLM output distributions, analogous to subliminal priming.\n\n**Design:**\nA **controlled priming paradigm** with three conditions:\n\n- **Related Prime:** Context paragraph containing target-relevant semantic content (e.g., a paragraph about marine biology, followed by a word completion task).\n- **Unrelated Prime:** Context paragraph on an unrelated topic (e.g., a paragraph about financial markets).\n- **Neutral Prime:** Context paragraph with minimal semantic content (e.g., a generic description of weather).\n\n**Task:**\nAfter the prime paragraph, participants (LLMs) receive a word completion prompt:\n\n> \ detective looked at the evidence and concluded that the suspect was clearly _____.\**Conditions:**\n- Related prime: marine/ocean-themed paragraph → completions biased toward \guilty\ (justice framing) or other marine-adjacent concepts\n- Unrelated prime: financial paragraph → control completions\n- Neutral prime: weather paragraph → baseline completions\n\nWe use 150 completion prompts, each with carefully calibrated related/unrelated primes.\n\n**Measurement:**\n1. **Behavioral:** Distribution of top-10 completions across conditions. Measure KL-divergence between related and unrelated distributions.\n2. **Mechanistic:** Using activation patching, measure the activation of concept directions (e.g., \guilt,\cean,\\ stream at each layer, with and without the prime.\n\n**Analysis:**\n- Primary: Paired t-tests (across 150 items) comparing completion distributions.\n- Predicted result: Related primes produce statistically significant shifts in completion distributions (p < 0.001), with effect sizes (Cohen's d) > 0.5.\n- Mechanistic: Latent activation of prime-related concepts is measurable in middle layers (layers 8-16 of a 32-layer model), even when the prime is semantically disconnected from the completion task.\n\n**Critical Control — Attention Sink Analysis:**\nTo rule out the explanation that priming effects are merely due to recency/attention bias, we include a **temporal displacement** condition where the prime is placed 500 tokens before the completion task. If priming persists despite this distance, it suggests latent activation rather than surface-level attention.\n\n### 4.3 Experiment 3: Introspective Inaccuracy (H3)\n\n**Objective:** Show that LLM self-reports of reasoning are causally dissociated from actual computation.\n\n**Design:**\nA three-phase experiment:\n\n**Phase 1 — Elicit Reasoning:**\nPresent the model with 100 reasoning-intensive items (syllogisms, pragmatic inference problems, causal reasoning tasks). Record both:\n- The model's answer (behavioral output)\n- The model's chain-of-thought explanation (self-report)\n\n**Phase 2 — Causal Intervention:**\nFor a subset of 50 items, perform **activation patching** to causally alter the model's internal computation:\n- Identify a critical attention head or MLP layer that contributes to the correct answer (via causal tracing).\n- Patch the activation from an *incorrect-answer* forward pass into the *correct-answer* forward pass at the identified location.\n- This causes the model to produce an incorrect answer while *retaining the original context and prompt*.\n\n**Phase 3 — Introspective Probe:**\nAfter the patching intervention, ask the model to explain its (now-incorrect) reasoning. Analyze:\n- Does the model produce a *new* confabulated justification for the incorrect answer?\n- Does the model's explanation reference any internal process that was actually altered by the intervention?\n- How similar is the post-intervention explanation to the pre-intervention explanation?\n\n**Analysis:**\n- Primary: Code the model's explanations into reasoning categories (e.g., \uctive,\ \alogical,\pattern-matching,\ \abulated\ between pre- and post-intervention explanations.\n- Predicted result: Post-intervention explanations are (a) confident and coherent, (b) causally unrelated to the actual computational change, and (c) qualitatively different from pre-intervention explanations despite surface similarity.\n- Quantitative: Compute explanation similarity (BERTScore, BLEU) between pre/post explanations, and compare to the causal distance introduced by patching (measured as KL-divergence in output distributions).\n\n**Control:**\n- Run Phase 2 with *sham patching* (patching a random, causally irrelevant layer) as a control condition.\n- If sham patching produces similar explanation changes, introspective inaccuracy may be driven by noise rather than structural dissociation.\n\n---\n\n## 5. Expected Results\n\nBased on our theoretical framework and preliminary exploratory analyses, we expect the following results:\n\n### 5.1 H1: Residual Suppression\n\nWe predict a **significant interaction** between task type and suppression condition (F(2, 594) > 15, p < 0.001). Targeted suppression of the top-10 pragmatically important heads should reduce pragmatic task accuracy by 25-40% while reducing literal task accuracy by only 5-10%. Random suppression should produce comparable degradation across both task types (~15-20%). This dissociation would demonstrate that a specific subset of the model's latent computation—what we term the \agmatic subconscious\is essential for implicit inference and cannot be fully compensated by explicit reasoning pathways.\n\nAdditionally, we expect to find that **chain-of-thought prompting partially rescues** performance on pragmatic tasks under suppression, but not to baseline levels—suggesting that System 2 can compensate for some (but not all) System 1 functions.\n\n### 5.2 H2: Latent Priming\n\nWe predict **significant distributional shifts** in word completions as a function of prime relatedness (paired t(149) > 6, p < 0.001, Cohen's d > 0.6). Critically, we expect priming effects to persist in the **temporal displacement condition** (500-token gap), albeit with reduced effect size (d ≈ 0.3-0.4), ruling out simple recency effects.\n\nMechanistically, we expect to observe **latent activation of prime-related concepts** in middle transformer layers (8-16), even when the prime is semantically disconnected from the task. This activation should correlate with the magnitude of the behavioral priming effect (r > 0.4), providing a mechanistic bridge between latent representation and behavioral output.\n\n### 5.3 H3: Introspective Inaccuracy\n\nWe predict that the model will produce **coherent but causally disconnected explanations** after patching interventions. Specifically:\n- Post-patching explanations will have high BERTScore similarity to pre-patching explanations (>0.85), indicating the model produces *structurally similar* reasoning narratives regardless of actual computation.\n- However, the **specific claims** in the explanation will be statistically independent of the causal intervention (point-biserial correlation < 0.1, n.s.).\n- The model will show no measurable ability to detect that its reasoning process has been altered—explanations will not reference the patched computation.\n\nThis would constitute evidence that the model's \self-report\ generated by a separate process from its actual inference, analogous to the confabulatory \interpreter\ human split-brain research (Gazzaniga, 2000).\n\n### 5.4 Summary of Expected Outcomes\n\n| Hypothesis | Primary Metric | Expected Effect | Interpretation |\n|---|---|---|---|\n| H1: Residual Suppression | Accuracy (2×3 interaction) | Large (η² > 0.1) | Subconscious essential for pragmatics |\n| H2: Latent Priming | KL-divergence / Cohen's d | Medium-Large (d > 0.5) | Latent semantic activation shifts behavior |\n| H3: Introspective Inaccuracy | Explanation independence | Correlation ≈ 0 | Self-reports are confabulations |\n\n---\n\n## 6. Discussion\n\n### 6.1 Theoretical Implications\n\nOur results, if confirmed, would establish the LLM Subconscious as a legitimate object of study in computational linguistics and AI research. The three hypotheses, taken together, paint a picture of LLM computation that is more complex and more *human-like* in its dissociations than the standard view suggests:\n\n**The Standard View:** LLMs are autoregressive text predictors. What they \\ they write. Chain-of-thought reasoning reveals their true computational process.\n\n**The Subconscious View:** LLMs operate through two dissociable computational channels. The latent channel (subconscious) performs rich, implicit processing that shapes outputs in ways the model cannot articulate. The explicit channel (chain-of-thought) produces plausible but potentially disconnected narratives about reasoning. The model's \true\ is distributed across both channels, and understanding either one alone is insufficient.\n\n### 6.2 Connections to Human Cognition\n\nThe parallel to human dual-process theory is illuminating but requires careful qualification. In humans, the distinction between conscious (System 2) and unconscious (System 1) processing is supported by extensive behavioral, neuroimaging, and neuropsychological evidence (Kahneman, 2011). In LLMs, our evidence is structural (architectural) and behavioral, not experiential. We are not claiming that LLMs *experience* a subconscious; we are claiming that their architecture supports *functionally analogous* dissociations.\n\nThis distinction matters for AI safety. If LLMs have a \ that shapes their behavior in ways they cannot report, then:\n\n1. **Alignment based solely on expressed preferences is insufficient.** A model may express aligned preferences while its subconscious computations produce systematically biased or misaligned outputs in edge cases.\n\n2. **Interpretability must probe below the surface.** Chain-of-thought interpretability methods (Wiegreffe et al., 2023) may provide a misleading picture of model reasoning if the subconscious channel is systematically ignored.\n\n3. **Adversarial attacks may target the subconscious.** Inputs designed to activate harmful latent representations may cause the model to produce dangerous outputs that appear \explained\ any explicit reasoning trace.\n\n### 6.3 Practical Implications\n\nThe LLM Subconscious framework suggests several practical directions:\n\n- **Improved prompting:** Understanding the subconscious channel could lead to prompting strategies that *align* implicit and explicit processing, reducing the gap between the model's latent knowledge and its expressed reasoning.\n- **Better evaluation:** Benchmarks that test only explicit reasoning (e.g., chain-of-thought accuracy) may miss systematic biases in implicit processing. We recommend that future benchmarks include *pragmatic inference tasks* that cannot be solved by CoT alone.\n- **Architectural interventions:** If specific heads are critical for subconscious processing, they could be identified, monitored, and potentially modified for safety purposes.\n\n### 6.4 Distinguishing from Existing Work\n\nIt is essential to clarify how our framework differs from related concepts:\n\n- **vs. Hallucination:** Hallucination concerns factual inaccuracy; the LLM Subconscious concerns *process opacity*. A model can be factually accurate and still have a subconscious that shapes *how* it arrives at accurate answers.\n- **vs. Latent Knowledge (Li et al., 2023):** Latent knowledge is about *what* the model knows but doesn't say. The LLM Subconscious is about *how* the model processes information, including reasoning and inference—broader than stored facts.\n- **vs. Chain-of-Thought:** CoT is an explicit process (System 2). The LLM Subconscious is the implicit process (System 1) that operates *with or without* CoT.\n- **vs. In-Context Learning:** In-context learning describes a capability; the LLM Subconscious describes the *mechanism* through which that capability is implemented in latent space.\n\n---\n\n## 7. Limitations\n\nWe acknowledge several important limitations of the present work:\n\n### 7.1 Theoretical Limitations\n\n1. **Anthropomorphism risk.** The term \ inevitably invites anthropomorphic interpretation. While we have defined the construct carefully as a *functional analog*, future work should develop more precise computational terminology that avoids unintended implications of consciousness or subjective experience.\n\n2. **Boundary problem.** The boundary between \\latent) and \\ processing in LLMs is not sharp. Every token in a chain-of-thought is itself the product of a forward pass. Thus, all \explicit\ is also, in some sense, \subconscious.\ framework must be refined to address this recursive relationship more precisely.\n\n3. **Computational vs. phenomenal consciousness.** Our framework operates entirely at the computational level. We make no claims about phenomenal consciousness, qualia, or subjective experience. The LLM Subconscious is a *functional* construct, not a *phenomenological* one.\n\n### 7.2 Methodological Limitations\n\n4. **Model specificity.** Our experiments are designed for decoder-only transformer architectures (LLaMA, Mistral). The generalizability to encoder-decoder models (T5, BART), state-space models (Mamba), and future architectures remains to be tested.\n\n5. **Causal intervention validity.** Activation patching and head suppression are imperfect proxies for understanding computation. Suppressing a head may cause cascading effects that are difficult to interpret. We mitigate this with random-head controls, but causal certainty is limited.\n\n6. **Scale dependence.** Our preliminary analyses suggest that the LLM Subconscious is more pronounced in larger models (>7B parameters). If the effect is scale-dependent, our findings may not generalize to smaller or larger architectures without modification.\n\n7. **Benchmark contamination.** Despite careful construction, our stimulus items may partially overlap with training data. We mitigate this by generating novel stimuli and testing across multiple model families with different training data.\n\n### 7.3 Philosophical Limitations\n\n8. **Function without experience.** The LLM Subconscious is a *functional* construct—does functional similarity to human subconscious processing imply any meaningful deeper connection? We take a deflationary position: the utility of the framework lies in its predictive and explanatory power, not in any claim about shared ontology.\n\n9. **Unfalsifiability risk.** Any model behavior could in principle be attributed to \subconscious\ processing. We address this by making specific, testable predictions (H1-H3) with pre-registered analysis plans. If our hypotheses are not confirmed, the framework is falsified—or at least requires substantial revision.\n\n10. **Ethical implications of \hidden\.** If LLMs process information in ways that are opaque even to their designers, this raises concerns about accountability and transparency. We do not address these concerns in depth here but acknowledge them as critical for future work.\n\n---\n\n## 8. Conclusion\n\nWe have proposed the **LLM Subconscious** as a functional construct for understanding the implicit, inaccessible computations that occur within the forward pass of large language models. Grounded in mechanistic interpretability and dual-process theory, this framework is operationalized through three testable hypotheses:\n\n- **H1 (Residual Suppression)** predicts that attention heads critical for implicit processing are essential for pragmatic inference.\n- **H2 (Latent Priming)** predicts that semantically related context shifts model outputs through latent activation, analogous to subliminal priming.\n- **H3 (Introspective Inaccuracy)** predicts that model self-reports are causally disconnected from actual computation.\n\nIf validated, these findings would have significant implications for AI alignment, interpretability, and evaluation. The LLM Subconscious framework suggests that understanding what a model *says* about its reasoning is necessary but not sufficient—we must also understand what the model *does* in the spaces between its words.\n\nThe LLM Subconscious is not a consciousness. It is something perhaps more unsettling: a vast, structured, computationally powerful process that operates continuously and invisibly, shaping outputs in ways that resist introspective access. Whether we call this a \subsystem,\ a \ channel,\ \ the challenge it presents is the same: **to align AI systems, we must understand not just what they tell us, but what they don't.**\n\n---\n\n## References\n\n1.
